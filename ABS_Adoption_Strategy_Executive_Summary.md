**Text on Slide**: 
"Awareness → Desire → Knowledge → Ability → Reinforcement"

**Speaker Notes** (Add to Notes pane):
Structured change management research tells us that successful adoption follows a predictable journey. CSAs must understand and guide customers through five critical stages. Stage 1 – Awareness: People need to understand why change is necessary. This starts with data and facts. Within ABS, we are close to are customers, and those we are closest to in the customer space are usually technical powerhouses.  They are slinging Power Platform solutions and developing the cutting edge prompts, but if you look at your customer's data and really see the forest for the trees you might see that there is indeed a gap in adoption in some of our products. This fact-based urgency moves people from "I didn't know this was a problem" to "I see the situation clearly." Stage 2 – Desire: Knowing about a problem doesn't create willingness to change. People need to want the change. This requires connecting change to personal and organizational benefits. A CSA helps customer leaders see how better adoption improves decision-making speed, reduces meeting time, improves employee satisfaction. We appeal to intrinsic motivation. Stage 3 – Knowledge: Once people want to change, they need to know how. This is where training comes in, but it's targeted and timed for maximum impact. Rather than generic training for everyone, we provide role-specific guidance at the moment of need. Sales teams learn how Teams accelerates pipeline management; finance teams learn how Power BI improves reporting efficiency. Stage 4 – Ability: Knowledge isn't enough; people need practice and reinforcement to build confidence and competence. Champions, peer support, and ongoing coaching are critical. A customer's first attempt at using a new process feels awkward – like writing with your non-dominant hand. Through repeated practice with support, the new behavior becomes natural and efficient. Stage 5 – Reinforcement: Once people are using new tools effectively, they need ongoing reinforcement to sustain change and prevent regression. This includes celebrating successes, recognizing early adopters, measuring progress, maintaining communication about benefits being realized. Without reinforcement, people often drift back to old habits. CSAs who understand this journey can diagnose where adoption is stalling and apply the right intervention. If adoption is low due to lack of awareness, training won't help – you need better communication of the business case. If desire is the barrier, you need to address concerns and build leadership sponsorship. This diagnostic capability transforms CSAs into true change strategists.
